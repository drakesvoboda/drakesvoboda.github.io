Dec. 2020

Natural Language Inference

For the final project in my natural language processing class, my group and I were tasked with solving three natural language inference (NLI) benchmarks:

CommonsenseQA—given a common-sense question, choose the correct answer out of 5 multiple-choice options

Conversational Entailment—given a short dialoge between speakers, determine if a given hypothesis can be inferred from the dialog.

Everyday Actions Text (EAT)—determine if a short story is plausible, and if it isn't, determine when the story stops making sense.

Natural language inference is the problem of determining if a hypothesis can be reasonably infered from a natural language premise. NLI is a notoriously difficult problem that requires machines to reason about the meanings of language. Deep-learning has been a huge breakthrough in NLP research and has advanced the state-of-the-art for many tasks. Not-surprisingly, large transformer models like BERT were the best performers amongst the groups in the class. My group and I made use of transformers and—out of twenty-five teams in the class—performed fifth best on the Conversational Entailment benchmark and third best performance on the EAT benchmark.

Dec. 2020

Static Branch Prediction for LLVM IRs Using Machine Learning

For the final project in my advanced compilers class, my group and I trained an ML model to predict the bias of an assembly branch instruction. Static-branch prediction is the process of predicting which direction a branch instruction will take before it is executed. Accuracte branch prediction is really important for compilers to make intelligent decisions when optimizing code. Our model performs better than LLVM's internal branch predictor, which we think is a really good result.

Dec. 2018

Deep.ditch: Computer Vision for Automated Road Damage Detection

This project was my first experience learning about and applying machine learning. I became interested in ML as a junior in college and I asked one of my professors and mentors (Dr. Shiyong Lu) if knew of any interesting projects to work on. He pointed me towards a vision competition hosted by a workshop at the 2018 IEEE Conference on Big Data.

The goal of the competion was to detect and localize instances of road damage in images. I taught myself deep-learning and pytorch, trained my model, wrote a report, and submitted my results. I placed 10th overall in the competition and my report was accepted by the workshop's reviewers (not bad for an undergrad working alone). Unfortunetly, I was unable to attend the conference and my report went unpublished, but hey, I was just happy it got accepted.

My senior year, my capstone project team deployed my model to an iOS app that automatically reports road damage. The model runs locally on a mobile device and is used to capture images when it detects them and store the images, along with their gps coordinates, to a remote database. The idea was that municipalities, who currently rely on individual reports and hired experts, could mount smartphones to fleets of vehicles to passivly and automatically locate and report road damage.
